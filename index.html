GTM Engineering / The Mindset

The GTM Engineer Mindset

Augmenting the human sales experience by creating a system is the goal. Not replacing the human seller and not automating everything. Giving the right person the right infrastructure so they can do the one thing that actually moves revenue, which is talk to the right customers at the right time.

What GTM Engineering Actually Is

GTM engineering is the bridge between sales and systems. When those two things work in harmony the business scales. When they do not, companies hire another rep and wonder why nothing changes.

A lot of the work that individual sellers technically own, sourcing leads, writing messaging, prioritizing accounts, doing research, is work that most sellers would rather not do. It is not the part of the job that brings a true seller joy and it is not where they create the most value. The highest leverage thing a seller can do is talk to customers at the right time. GTM engineering takes everything else off their plate so they can do exactly that.

How I Got Here

For years as a SaaS AE I also wanted to be the person who built the sales team. I love building things and have been relentlessly curious in finding new ways to be better at my job technically speaking. If I was going to make cold calls and craft emails all day, I wanted to give myself the best possible opportunity to succeed.

In early 2025 I connected with the founder of a Salesforce consulting firm while I was still working as an AE. The goal was to build the entire go to market function from scratch. No lists, no sequences, no defined ICP, no CRM structure. Most people would have seen that as a problem. I saw it as exactly the kind of challenge I had been looking for.

By late 2025 I made the jump. The goal was to build and systemize a go to market process so that this firm could grow, generate revenue, and eventually reach a point where the founder had options to sell. That work is ongoing and the results are great. $250K in closed ARR, $700K in pipeline built. An end to end co-sell motion built from zero with Salesforce AEs across multiple verticals. A signal-based outreach system built in Clay targeting fractional leaders starting new engagements at Salesforce accounts. A direct to business motion leveraged heavily on leading with an AppExchange product that ties in with selling a managed service consulting package for the larger Salesforce org.

Building, creating, and understanding how systems and people work together to produce outcomes that neither could produce alone.

Why GTM Engineering Makes Startups More Money

The fastest way to scale revenue at an early stage company is not hiring more reps. It is building the system that makes the reps you already have dramatically more effective. One rep working a clean scored list with signal-based outreach and a properly structured CRM will outperform three reps working off gut instinct every time. When you fix the system underneath the sales team you get more pipeline from the same headcount, shorter sales cycles because you are reaching the right people at the right time, and a CRM that actually reflects reality so forecasting stops being a guessing game.

All go to market tactics expire. Today they expire faster than they ever have because AI makes everything easy to copy. The moment something works everyone learns about it and the tooling makes it easy to replicate. A startup without a centralized GTM system has no way to experiment fast enough to stay ahead of that. A startup with one can test a new approach, measure what happened, and iterate before the market catches up.

That ability to keep finding new edges continuously is worth more than any single tactic because it does not expire. The right human and the right system working together is what sustains a competitive advantage over time. One without the other is not enough to be the best. Augmenting the human sales experience by creating a system is the goal.

Why AI GTM Pilots Fail

Most companies approach AI in go to market the wrong way. They either give the AI too much control or they expect it to do the wrong things entirely.

Giving the AI too much control looks like deploying an AI SDR, pointing it at a target account list, and telling it to go send emails. That works for a while and then it stops working because you have burned your domain, tired out your market, and produced messaging that has no real context behind it. The AI does not know that the engineering leader who came up through backend ops is three times more likely to respond than the one who came up through front end development. A human SDR who has been trained on your ICP knows that and develops sound judgement by experience. An AI that has been handed a rough description of your buyer does not.

Expecting AI to do the wrong things looks like asking it to monitor your reply rates and automatically adjust your messaging based on what is working. That sounds smart but it is actually putting AI in the wrong place. AI is excellent at generating the first message. It is excellent at finding data points on accounts and contacts. It is excellent at the basic work of data labeling, data cleaning, and enrichment that nobody wants to do but that everything else depends on. It is not good at analyzing a campaign and making copy adjustments because there are too many factors it cannot see. Brand perception, product reputation, account history, market timing. A good demand gen person will do that better every time. Any system with a feedback loop should have a human at the back end of that loop.

The companies getting real results from AI in go to market are not the ones chasing the flashy agentic workflows. They are the ones doing the boring work first. Clean data, proper industry segmentation, correctly scored account lists. When that foundation is in place everything built on top of it works better automatically.

Why I Am Different

I have seven years of B2B SaaS sales experience. I have been a quota carrying rep my whole career and know what it feels like to be on the highest of highs and lowest of lows in terms of carrying quota.

A GTM engineer who has never carried a number builds for an idea of what a rep needs. A GTM engineer who has carried a number builds for the reality of what a rep needs. Those are different things and the output shows it. You know which shortcuts create problems downstream. You know what a rep will actually use versus what looks good in a demo. You know where the system needs to be human and where it needs to be automated. That field level intuition is not something you learn from a course or a podcast. You get it from sitting in the AE seat for years, owning a number, and feeling the pressure of an end of quarter push to hit your goal.

RevOps vs GTM Engineering

People confuse these two all the time and it costs companies money.

RevOps is a strategy and finance adjacent role. They own forecasting, pipeline coverage, territory design, and quota setting. They are answering the question of what the numbers should be. It is important work but it is mostly analytical. They are not building the systems that generate the pipeline. They are measuring and reporting on it.

GTM engineering is a builder role. I am not sitting in a dashboard analyzing what went wrong last quarter. I am building the system that makes sure next quarter does not have the same problem. The workflows, the data layer, the signals, the automations, the sequences. The infrastructure that a sales team runs on every single day.

The easiest way to say it is this. RevOps tells you the territory carve is wrong. I fix the data that caused the bad territory carve and build the system so it does not happen again.

Most early stage companies blur these two functions together and end up with one person doing neither job well. The analytical work crowds out the building work. The ticket queue never gets cleared because there is no system underneath it. Nothing compounds because nothing is actually being built from the ground up.

RevOps and GTM engineering work best when they are separate but connected. RevOps sets the targets. GTM engineering builds the infrastructure that hits them. Without both of these teams, it is incredibly difficult for an organization to be innovative.

The GTM Stack

Most companies end up with too many tools that do not talk to each other. The rep lives across fifteen tabs, data lives in five different places, and nothing syncs correctly. The result is bad data, missed signals, and a sales team spending more time managing tools than talking to customers.

Every tool in the stack has to earn its place by doing one job well and passing clean data to everything else. If it does not do both it does not belong in the stack.

CRM
Pipeline management and source of truth. Every contact, account, deal, and activity lives here. If the CRM has bad data everything downstream produces bad outcomes.
Data Enrichment and Prospecting
List building, contact finding, firmographic and technographic data. This is where the golden list gets built. Without this layer you are prospecting blind.
GTM Orchestration
Connecting data sources, building workflows, and automating across the stack. The connective tissue of the entire stack. This is where most of the GTM engineering work actually happens.
Sequencing and Sales Engagement
Email, LinkedIn, and phone outreach automation. The sequencing tool is only as good as the list it is working from. Garbage in, garbage out.
Conversation Intelligence
Call recording, transcription, and analysis. This data feeds the follow-up automation and content generation layers. Without it the post-call workflows have nothing to work from.
Intent and Signal Data
Identifying accounts that are in market or showing buying behavior. This is what separates a good list from a high intent list.
Content and Document Generation
Automating decks, proposals, follow-up emails, and one-pagers. The system generates the first draft. The rep reviews and sends. What used to take an hour takes five minutes.
Business Intelligence and Reporting
Dashboards, forecasting, and pipeline visibility. If you cannot measure it you cannot improve it.
Data Warehouse
Storing and querying large volumes of GTM data at scale. Necessary when data volume outgrows what a CRM can handle cleanly.
Communication and Rep Interface
Where reps actually live day to day. The goal is one screen that surfaces everything the rep needs without opening another tab.

Every tool in the stack serves the rep. If it does not make the rep more effective it does not belong.

The Current State of Sales Teams

The average sales rep today is not spending most of their day selling. They are spending it on everything else.

Updating the CRM after every call. Doing account research manually before they can even pick up the phone. Staring at a list of accounts trying to figure out who to call first and what to say when they do. Sitting through pipeline reviews where managers ask for status updates instead of strategizing on how to close deals. Building post-call decks and follow-up emails from scratch after every meeting. Compiling proposals by hand. Waiting on deal desk. Hitting bottlenecks because the process depends on a person instead of a system. Responding slowly to new inbound leads because there is no structure around it. Getting pulled into customer service issues that have nothing to do with revenue. Who else at the company talked to this customer and why does the rep not know about it. And at the end of all of it, wondering why they did not hit their number.

The rep is not the problem. The environment is the problem. Too many systems that do not talk to each other. Too much admin work that should not exist. Too much time spent on low value tasks that a well built system could handle automatically. Analysis paralysis from having too much information with no clear signal on what actually matters today.

When a rep spends four hours a day on admin work they have four fewer hours to do the one thing that actually drives revenue. Multiply that across an entire sales team and the business is leaving an enormous amount of money on the table before a single call is made.

Future State of GTM When Working With Me

The result is a single pane of glass. One interface and everything a rep needs to do their job at the highest level, surfaced at the right moment without them having to go find it. A rep should not live across five Chrome tabs with information scattered that does not work cohesively. They show up, open one screen, and know exactly who to talk to, what to say, and why today is the right time to reach out. No analysis paralysis. No digging through tools to figure out what happened on the last call or who else at the company has talked to this account. The system knows and it surfaces what matters. The rep executes.

SDRs and AEs are not replaced. They are repurposed to do more strategic selling. The goal is to give them back their edge. To take someone managing 100 accounts and give them the infrastructure to manage 1000 without burning out. To remove the noise so the only thing left is what actually matters, connecting with the right people and closing deals.

My job is to make sure none of that gets buried under bad data, generic AI messaging, and systems that do not talk to each other. Here is how it gets built.

Pre-sales and pipeline generation

It starts with the golden list. Before any outreach happens the ICP has to be defined with precision. Not a rough sketch of who the product is for but a scored, tiered, living list of the exact accounts that match, ranked by fit, enriched with the right contacts, and updated continuously as the market moves. Most companies skip this step or do it once and never revisit it. That is where the pipeline problems start.

Once the golden list exists the system does the pre-sales work automatically. The right accounts are identified, the right contacts are surfaced, and the outreach is personalized at scale. This is not generic messaging blasted at a list. It is targeted outreach built on real signals, company data, job changes, intent triggers, and firmographic fit, crafted to reach the right person at the moment they are most likely to respond. The rep's job is to have the conversation. My job is to make sure they are always talking to the right person at the right time.

Signal-based workflows

The system watches accounts continuously. Product usage patterns, intent signals, contract milestones, job changes, and internal activity across the team. When a colleague had a conversation with an account last week the rep knows about it before they pick up the phone. When something happens that requires action it surfaces automatically with the context already attached and a suggested next step. The rep does not have to go looking for the signal. The signal finds them. This only works when the data underneath it is clean and synchronized across every tool in the stack. If the CRM and the data enrichment layer and the sequencing platform are not talking to each other correctly the whole thing breaks down silently. Data synchronization is not a nice to have. It is what makes every other workflow actually execute.

Follow-up automation

After a discovery call the system drafts the follow-up email based on what was actually said on the call. Not a generic template. A draft that reflects the specific conversation, the specific pain, and the specific next step. The rep reviews it, adjusts if needed, and sends. What used to take thirty minutes takes three.

Content generation

When a deal closes the post-sales team should not be scrambling to build a kickoff deck from scratch. The system pulls account data, relevant call recordings, and any usage or product information available, and formats all of it into a deck that is ready before the first call. The team shows up and presents. The same logic applies to QBRs. The deck builds itself from usage data and conversation history. Nobody is spending three hours the night before assembling slides.

GTM Engineering / First 90 Days

The First 90 Days

Most GTM engineers walk into a new company and immediately start touching tools. They audit the tech stack, they look at sequences, they start building before they understand what they are actually building for. That is the wrong order of operations. The first month is not about building anything. It is about understanding everything.

Days 1 to 30: Understand Before You Build

The first question I am trying to answer when I walk through the door is simple. Why are you winning and why are you losing. Everything else flows from that.

Before a single workflow gets touched I want to understand the product or service at a deep level. Not the pitch deck version. The real version. Why do customers actually buy it. Is this a must have or a nice to have. How sticky is it once it is in the door. How complicated is the education cycle from first conversation to close. Does the ideal customer instantly understand what the product does or does it require a significant amount of explaining before they see the value. Where are deals getting lost in the process and is that a targeting problem, a messaging problem, or a product problem.

I want to understand who has already bought. I spend a significant amount of time going through every closed won deal and asking the same questions. What title did this person hold. Where were they from. What pain were they experiencing before they found us. What made them take a meeting in the first place. What made them introduce the product to their leadership team. What made them sign. That pattern is the ICP and I want to define it with enough precision that there is no margin for error when we go back to market.

I also want to understand the competitive landscape in full. What do competitors have that we do not. What do we have that competitors do not. Can we actually win the deals we are going after or are we showing up to fights we cannot win. Before any message goes to a prospect I want to know how they perceive our brand. Are they confused by the website. Does our positioning instantly click or does it require education. What does a prospect think when they see our name for the first time.

Then I look at the sales team. How are reps getting their leads today. Are they performing too many jobs besides selling. How complicated is their day to day. Are they working out of a single place or are they living across fifteen tabs. What tools are in place and are they actually working together or just sitting next to each other with no real integration. Is every conversation being recorded. Is signaling and intent data being used to find new leads or is everything manual. Who wrote the outreach messaging and is it actually good or is it AI slop that no one would respond to. Is leadership looking at a dashboard that tells them what is actually happening or are they operating on gut feel and anecdote.

I am not building anything in month one. I am embedding myself into every detail of how and why the system is operating the way it is today. That context is what separates a GTM engineer who builds the right thing from one who builds something impressive that nobody uses.

Days 31 to 60: Define and Document

By the end of month one I have enough context to start making decisions. Month two is about turning that context into a foundation.

The ICP gets defined in full. Not a rough sketch. A scored, documented, living definition of exactly who we sell to, why they buy, what signals indicate they are ready, and what disqualifies an account before we waste time on it. This document becomes the north star for every targeting and messaging decision that follows.

The golden list gets built. A tiered, enriched list of the best target accounts in the market built on the ICP definition, layered with intent signals, job change data, firmographic fit, and technographic indicators. This is the first real deliverable and it changes how the sales team operates immediately.

But the golden list is not just a prospecting tool. It is the source of truth that makes everything else possible. Without it a company cannot be nimble. It cannot pivot quickly when the market shifts, respond to a competitor making a move, or run a new experiment with any confidence because there is no clean foundation to run it against. Every tactical decision becomes slower and more expensive because the team is always starting from scratch with incomplete or outdated information.

The list is built to be maintained and iterated on over time because the intelligence that powers it is always evolving. The market moves, signals change, new buying patterns emerge, and the ICP sharpens as more deals are won and lost. A company that keeps that list current and accurate can test a new message, a new vertical, or a new motion and know within weeks whether it is working because the foundation underneath the experiment is solid. A company without it is always guessing, always rebuilding, and always slower than it should be. The golden list is not the first project. It is the infrastructure that makes every other project work.

The full tech stack audit gets completed and documented. Every tool in the stack gets evaluated against one question. Is it doing its job and is it passing clean data to everything else. The audit does not just identify what is broken. It identifies what is possible. What could the system produce if the right infrastructure was in place. That analysis goes to leadership as a formal presentation. This is why your team is performing the way they are. This is what is costing you. This is what it looks like when it is fixed.

Days 61 to 90: Build the Infrastructure

Month three is when the building starts. The ICP is defined. The golden list exists. The audit is complete and leadership has seen it. Now the work is connecting everything correctly so the system produces what it is supposed to produce.

The orchestration layer gets built first. The tools need to talk to each other. The CRM needs clean data flowing into it from the enrichment layer. The sequencing platform needs to be pulling from the golden list, not a random export from six months ago. The conversation intelligence tool needs to be feeding the follow-up automation. Every workflow needs to be documented so that when something breaks someone can fix it without it becoming a crisis.

The first sequences go out against the golden list with messaging that was built on what we learned in month one. Not templates. Not AI generated copy that sounds like every other email in the prospect's inbox. Messaging that reflects a genuine understanding of who this person is, what they care about, and why this product is relevant to them right now.

By day 90 the company does not feel the same as it did on day one. The reps know exactly what accounts to work and why those accounts were chosen. The messaging reflects a genuine understanding of the buyer rather than a generic template that could have been written for anyone. The tools are connected and passing clean data to each other so nothing falls through the cracks between systems. Leadership has a dashboard that reflects what is actually happening in the pipeline rather than what someone thinks is happening based on a conversation from last week.

None of this is finished at day 90. The work is never finished. But the mindset has shifted, the momentum is building, and the urgency to keep improving is now embedded in how the team operates every day. That is the real deliverable. Not a completed system but an organization that now knows what good looks like and is moving toward it with intention.

And that momentum does not stay contained to one function. Once the infrastructure is in place and the data is clean, every part of the business that touches the customer starts to operate differently. Marketing, customer success, product. The same foundation that fixes the sales motion ends up being the thing the entire organization builds on top of.

The Stack

Apollo for list building and persona management. Clay for enrichment and signal detection. Salesforce rebuilt from scratch for pipeline management. Reply.io for multi-touch sequencing across email and LinkedIn.
